## Manuscript

The temporal specificity of salinity effects on growth onset reveals critical phenological sensitivity windows that align with rice developmental biology. Among monthly environmental variables spanning May through August, only early-season salinity exhibited consistently strong positive relationships with growth onset timing across both long-term and recent analysis periods, while August salinity showed no relationship. This narrow temporal window corresponds precisely to the early vegetative growth phase in rice, particularly the tillering stage occurring approximately three to five weeks after transplanting. During this critical period, the crop establishes its productive tiller population, which ultimately determines panicle number and yield potential. Salinity stress during tillering has been extensively documented to reduce tiller emergence and survival, directly constraining the vegetative foundation for subsequent reproductive development. The termination of salinity sensitivity in August reflects the phenological transition to reproductive primordium initiation, when tiller number becomes fixed and developmental priorities shift from vegetative expansion to panicle formation. This tight coupling between environmental sensitivity and developmental stage underscores why phenology-based approaches enable more precise temporal attribution of stress impacts than season-aggregated metrics.
