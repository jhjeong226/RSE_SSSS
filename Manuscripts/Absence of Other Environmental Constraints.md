## Manuscript

The weak relationships between growth onset timing and other environmental variables suggest that temperature, precipitation, and vapor pressure deficit remained within ranges that did not limit early vegetative growth during the study period. While these factors can theoretically affect rice phenology, their influence becomes apparent primarily when they deviate substantially from optimal ranges. In the study region, spring and early summer temperatures consistently supported active growth, precipitation patterns combined with irrigation management maintained adequate soil moisture, and vapor pressure deficit remained moderate. Under these conditions, salinity emerged as the dominant limiting factor because it represented the primary environmental stressor operating outside the range that management practices could fully ameliorate. This finding highlights an important principle in agricultural stress diagnosis. In systems where water and temperature are actively managed to support crop production, salinity or other non-managed stressors often control productivity variations because they represent uncorrected constraints. The satellite phenology approach effectively identifies these dominant constraints by revealing which environmental gradients correlate with observable developmental delays, enabling diagnostic focus on factors that actually limit performance rather than those that might theoretically matter but remain non-limiting in practice.
