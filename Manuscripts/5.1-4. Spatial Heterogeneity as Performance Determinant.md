## Manuscript

The contrasting predictive performance among cultivars reveals that spatial uniformity governs the effectiveness of satellite-based phenological metrics for yield estimation. When within-field growth shows high spatial variability, point-sampled yield measurements poorly represent the area-averaged phenological signals captured by satellite sensors. This mismatch introduces noise that weakens observable relationships between remotely sensed metrics and field-reported yields. The mechanism operates through scale incompatibility. Point sampling integrates information from a few square meters, while satellite pixels aggregate signals from hundreds to thousands of square meters. Under spatially uniform conditions, point samples adequately capture the area mean, permitting strong correspondence with satellite observations. However, under heterogeneous conditions, point samples may capture unrepresentative locations, introducing systematic bias or random error depending on sampling placement relative to within-field gradients. This sampling-sensor mismatch represents a fundamental constraint on validating satellite-derived agricultural metrics using conventional field measurement protocols. The observed relationship between cultivar-specific spatial heterogeneity and correlation strength confirms this mechanism, demonstrating that the satellite metric itself performs consistently but that validation outcomes depend critically on whether ground-truth data match the spatial scale of remote observations.
