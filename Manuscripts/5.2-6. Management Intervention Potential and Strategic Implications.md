## Manuscript

The complex regulation of senescence timing offers greater management flexibility compared to the irreversible nature of early-season delays. Once salinity stress has postponed growth onset and reduced early tiller establishment, the developmental deficit cannot be fully recovered through later interventions. The constrained tiller population limits panicle number regardless of subsequent favorable conditions, creating a persistent yield impact. In contrast, factors influencing senescence timing remain amenable to modification throughout much of the reproductive phase. Irrigation adjustments can extend grain-filling duration by maintaining leaf function, nutrient applications can delay senescence by preventing premature nitrogen limitation, and disease management can reduce stress-accelerated leaf senescence. This management responsiveness means that while early-season stress establishes fundamental constraints on yield potential, late-season management substantially determines the degree to which that potential is realized. From an operational perspective, these differential controls suggest that diagnostic attention and intervention resources should prioritize the May-July salinity window, when irreversible developmental limitations emerge, while recognizing that post-flowering management still offers opportunities to optimize outcomes within the constraints established by early-season conditions. The phenology-based framework enables this strategic temporal focus by identifying which developmental stages prove most sensitive to specific environmental stressors and therefore merit concentrated management effort.
