## Manuscript

The mechanism by which early-season salinity delays U90 timing primarily operates through osmotic and ionic stress effects on vegetative development. Elevated salinity in irrigation water reduces the water potential gradient between soil and roots, imposing osmotic stress that constrains water uptake even when water is physically abundant. This osmotic effect manifests rapidly within days of exposure, reducing cell expansion rates and overall vegetative growth. For rice in the tillering phase, this translates into slower leaf area development and delayed canopy closure, both of which are directly captured by satellite vegetation indices as delayed green-up timing. Beyond immediate osmotic effects, prolonged salinity exposure during early growth triggers ionic stress as sodium and chloride accumulate in tissues, disrupting metabolic processes and further suppressing growth rates. The coastal reclaimed land context of our study site amplifies these effects, as irrigation water sourced from estuarine reservoirs inherently carries elevated salinity that fluctuates with seasonal patterns of freshwater inflow and tidal intrusion. The strong positive correlation (higher salinity â†’ later U90) confirms that this stress-delay relationship operates consistently across inter-annual salinity variation. Importantly, the fact that correlations strengthened in the recent HLS period (r = 0.75-0.82) compared to the 23-year MODIS record (r = 0.55-0.62) suggests either increasing salinity stress intensity or greater consistency in this driver relative to other factors in recent years, warranting continued monitoring.
