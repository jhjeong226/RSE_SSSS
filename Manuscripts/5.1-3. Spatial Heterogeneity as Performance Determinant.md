## Manuscript

The contrasting predictive performance among cultivars reveals that spatial heterogeneity, rather than cultivation area size, governs the effectiveness of satellite-based phenological metrics for yield estimation. This finding reflects a fundamental scale incompatibility between point-based yield sampling and area-averaged satellite observations. Point sampling integrates information from a few square meters, while satellite pixels aggregate signals from hundreds to thousands of square meters. Under spatially homogeneous conditions, point samples adequately capture the area mean, permitting strong correspondence with satellite observations. Under heterogeneous conditions, however, point samples may capture unrepresentative locations, introducing noise that weakens observable correlations. This sampling-sensor mismatch represents a fundamental constraint on validating satellite-derived agricultural metrics using conventional field measurement protocols. Our finding that GQ achieved strong correlations despite its larger cultivation area, while SK showed weak correlations despite its smaller area, confirms that spatial heterogeneity, rather than field size, determines validation outcomes.
