## Manuscript

The superior performance of U90-D50 duration compared to peak vegetation indices fundamentally reflects the distinction between accumulated physiological processes and instantaneous canopy state. Peak vegetation indices such as NDVI and EVI capture maximum greenness at a single time point, explaining only 6% of yield variance in our analysis (Table 1). This weak performance occurs because maximum canopy development does not necessarily correspond to the phenological stages that determine final grain production. Rice yields depend not on achieving peak biomass, but on the duration of resource acquisition during grain-filling periods (Yang et al., 2008). In contrast, phenological duration metrics quantify the temporal extent of critical developmental windows. The U90-D50 interval specifically captures the length of the reproductive phase, during which panicle development, flowering, and grain filling occur. Our results demonstrate that this duration-based approach explains up to 76% of yield variance for individual cultivars (r = 0.874 for GQ), representing a thirteen-fold improvement over snapshot-based indices. This difference underscores a fundamental principle for satellite-based crop monitoring: yield formation is governed by temporal integration of physiological processes rather than peak instantaneous measurements.
