## Manuscript

Phenological duration metrics demonstrated substantial superiority over traditional snapshot-based approaches for yield estimation. While peak vegetation indices explained at most 6% of yield variance, the optimal duration metric (U90-D50) explained 27% across all cultivars and up to 76% for spatially uniform individual cultivars. This dramatic difference reflects a fundamental principle. Yield formation depends on accumulated resource capture over extended developmental periods rather than instantaneous biomass status. Peak greenness captures a single moment when canopy density reaches maximum, but provides no information about how long that productive canopy persists or when critical reproductive processes occur. In contrast, phenological duration quantifies the temporal extent of key growth stages, directly measuring the time available for photosynthetic carbon accumulation, nutrient uptake, and reproductive organ development. The physiological basis for this relationship has been established through field studies demonstrating that grain-filling duration, rather than rate, governs yield variation by determining total radiation and heat unit accumulation. Recent remote sensing research further validated this principle, showing that accumulated gross primary productivity during reproductive stages explains comparable variance levels to those observed in our duration-based analysis. The convergence of our satellite phenology findings with these physiological and process-based studies confirms that duration metrics capture yield-determining mechanisms rather than merely correlating with outcomes.
