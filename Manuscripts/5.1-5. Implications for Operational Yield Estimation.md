## Manuscript

Successful operational application of duration-based yield proxies requires preliminary assessment of spatial heterogeneity within target fields or management zones. Cultivars or growing conditions that produce spatially variable phenology necessitate either more intensive ground sampling to adequately represent field-level means or stratification into homogeneous sub-units for separate analysis. Satellite spatial resolution must also match the scale of management decisions. The 30-meter HLS resolution proved adequate for the relatively large fields in this study, but smaller or more fragmented agricultural systems might require finer resolution sensors or aggregation strategies that sacrifice spatial detail to improve signal reliability. Environmental monitoring data should similarly represent conditions experienced at the field scale rather than relying on distant weather stations that might not capture relevant microclimate or water quality variations. These operational requirements underscore that phenology-based agricultural monitoring represents a system-level approach requiring coordinated satellite, ground-truth, and environmental data collection designed to operate at compatible spatial and temporal scales. Meeting these requirements enables robust phenology-yield relationships, while mismatches in scale or sampling intensity introduce confounding factors that obscure observable patterns.
