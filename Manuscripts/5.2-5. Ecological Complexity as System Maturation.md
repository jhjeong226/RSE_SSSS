## Manuscript

The inability to identify a dominant driver for D50 timing should not be interpreted as a methodological limitation but rather as evidence of fundamental ecological complexity that emerges as growing systems mature. Early-season phenology (U90) occurs when environmental variables operate on a relatively simple system with limited confounding factors. At transplanting plus 4-6 weeks, crop status reflects primarily post-transplanting recovery and initial tillering, processes that respond predictably to contemporaneous environmental conditions. In contrast, senescence timing (D50) reflects the culmination of the entire season's dynamics, including early stress effects, compensatory growth responses, reproductive allocation trade-offs, and progressive nutrient mobilization. By the time crops reach senescence, they have experienced and integrated multiple environmental episodes across vegetative, reproductive, and grain-filling stages. This temporal accumulation creates equifinality, where multiple environmental pathways can lead to similar senescence timing. Additionally, senescence represents an active developmental program rather than a passive stress response, involving complex hormonal regulation (ethylene, abscisic acid) and source-sink dynamics that respond to whole-plant status rather than single environmental inputs. The observed threshold-dependent and nonlinear patterns likely reflect this complexity, in which D50 timing shifts only when multiple factors align or when individual stresses exceed critical thresholds that trigger accelerated senescence programs.
