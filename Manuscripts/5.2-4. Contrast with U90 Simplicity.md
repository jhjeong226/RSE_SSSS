## Manuscript

The absence of a single dominant driver for senescence timing contrasts sharply with the clear salinity control of growth onset, revealing fundamental differences in how environmental factors regulate early versus late phenological transitions. Growth onset represents a physiological threshold where accumulating favorable conditions trigger developmental progression from transplant establishment to active vegetative expansion. This threshold-crossing mechanism creates sensitivity to factors that either delay or accelerate reaching the requisite physiological state, making dominant single-factor control plausible when one environmental variable strongly limits the rate of threshold approach. Senescence timing, conversely, reflects the integration of multiple accumulated effects throughout the entire growing season, including nutrient remobilization signals, source-sink relationships, hormonal regulation, and stress damage accumulation. These diverse processes interact in complex ways, with threshold effects, compensation mechanisms, and multiple alternative pathways all capable of influencing when the crop begins canopy breakdown. The lack of a single dominant environmental driver for D50 timing thus reflects inherent ecological complexity in late-season phenological regulation rather than inadequate analysis.
