## Manuscript

In stark contrast to the clear salinity dominance over U90 timing, analysis of D50 (senescence onset) revealed no single consistent environmental driver. After controlling for U90 influence through partial correlation, none of the 20 monthly environmental variables exhibited robust predictive relationships with senescence timing (Fig. 5). Even salinity, which so strongly governed growth onset, showed inconsistent patterns for senescence. The long-term MODIS record indicated that July salinity significantly accelerated senescence (r = -0.50, p = 0.016, n = 23), suggesting mid-season stress induces premature leaf aging. However, this relationship disappeared in the recent HLS period (r = 0.67, p = 0.145, non-significant), indicating temporal instability in this driver-response relationship. Similarly, other environmental variables, including temperature, precipitation, accumulated temperature, and vapor pressure deficit, showed weak, variable correlations that did not reach significance. This fundamental difference in environmental control between growth onset and senescence timing reflects a critical distinction: U90 represents an early system state with few intervening processes, while D50 represents the outcome of accumulated seasonal effects, management interventions, and complex physiological trade-offs.
