These findings demonstrate that phenology-based diagnostic frameworks can transition satellite remote sensing from correlation-based yield estimation toward mechanistic attribution of production constraints. The temporal specificity achieved (identifying May-July as the critical salinity window) enables strategic resource allocation, concentrating management interventions when and where they matter most. This represents a fundamental advancement over season-aggregated analyses that obscure actionable timing information. The spatial-temporal integration further enables differentiated management strategies. Regions exhibiting delayed-U90 patterns require infrastructure-level interventions to address irrigation water quality, while areas showing duration vulnerability without U90 delay indicate other constraints requiring alternative solutions. This mechanistic compartmentalization supports precision agriculture approaches tailored to specific constraint types rather than uniform treatments across heterogeneous landscapes. For coastal agricultural systems globally, the framework provides a template for diagnosing salinity impacts through remotely observable phenological responses. As climate change intensifies saltwater intrusion in deltaic and estuarine production zones, operational monitoring systems based on this diagnostic approach could enable early warning and targeted adaptation. The demonstration that infrastructure configuration (pumping station positioning) mediates the distribution of environmental stress underscores the importance of water management system design in determining field-level vulnerability patterns.
