## Manuscript

Several limitations constrain generalization and indicate priorities for future research. The cultivar-specific analysis relied on limited temporal replication (n = 7 years), though the consistency of strong correlations for spatially uniform cultivars and their agronomic interpretability provides confidence in the identified relationships. The single-site focus, while enabling detailed characterization of infrastructure-environment-phenology linkages, limits immediate transferability to other coastal systems with different salinity regimes, irrigation configurations, or cultivar portfolios. Point-based yield sampling (five quadrats per field) proved insufficient in heterogeneous cultivation areas, underscoring the need for denser sampling or remote-sensing-based yield estimation to fully exploit high-resolution satellite phenology. The diagnostic framework identified early-season salinity as the primary temporal driver but could not resolve the complex controls on senescence timing, which likely involve threshold effects and multi-factor interactions requiring mechanistic crop models for complete attribution. Future research should validate the framework across multiple coastal rice systems to assess generalizability, develop operational near-real-time monitoring systems that integrate phenology tracking with environmental data streams, and conduct intervention experiments to test whether targeted early-season salinity mitigation effectively extends reproductive duration and increases yields, as predicted by the diagnostic relationships. Additionally, exploring whether similar phenology-environment linkages operate in other salinity-stressed systems, such as coastal vegetable production or inland areas affected by secondary salinization, would establish the framework's broader applicability for climate adaptation planning.
