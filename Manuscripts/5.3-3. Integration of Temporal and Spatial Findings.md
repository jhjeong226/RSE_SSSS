## Manuscript

The spatial overlap between delayed growth onset zones and abbreviated duration zones demonstrates successful integration of temporal environmental attribution with spatial vulnerability mapping. Growth onset timing serves as a spatial proxy for early-season salinity exposure because the May-July salinity control identified through temporal analysis operates uniformly across the landscape. Regions where satellite-detected growth onset consistently lags behind district norms therefore indicate where irrigation water quality systematically impairs early vegetative development. The correspondence between these delayed-onset zones and zones exhibiting chronically short reproductive durations confirms that early salinity stress propagates through the entire growing season to ultimately constrain yield formation. Conversely, zones showing abbreviated durations without delayed onset suggest vulnerability driven by factors other than early salinity, such as nutrient limitations, soil physical properties, or pest pressure. This dual-metric spatial analysis thus partitions the landscape not only by vulnerability severity but also by causal mechanism, enabling more precise targeting of management interventions to address specific limiting factors in different zones.
