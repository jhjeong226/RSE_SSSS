## Manuscript

This spatial finding integrates seamlessly with the temporal analysis of environmental controls (Section 5.2.1). We identified May-July salinity as the dominant temporal driver of U90 timing, and the spatial correspondence between phenological patterns enables attribution of vulnerability causes. Crucially, substantial overlap between delayed-U90 zones (Fig. 7c, 7d, Clusters 5-6) and duration-vulnerable zones (Fig. 7a, 7b, Clusters 1-2) emerged specifically in southern regions, confirming that early-season salinity drives vulnerability in these areas. This overlap validates the causal mechanism where high early-season salinity exposure through downstream irrigation water systematically delays growth onset, consequently shortening reproductive duration and reducing yields. However, some duration-vulnerable areas showed normal U90 timing, indicating that constraints other than early-season salinity affect these regions. This spatial differentiation demonstrates the diagnostic power of integrating temporal and spatial analyses. Where delayed-U90 and shortened-duration patterns coincide, early-season salinity represents the attributable cause. Where duration vulnerability occurs without a U90 delay, other environmental or management factors must be responsible. This mechanistic compartmentalization enables targeted intervention strategies tailored to the specific constraints operating in each zone.
