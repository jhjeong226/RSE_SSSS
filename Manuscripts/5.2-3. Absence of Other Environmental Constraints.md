The weak or non-significant relationships between U90 timing and other environmental variables (temperature, precipitation, vapor pressure deficit) reveal that these more commonly documented factors do not primarily limit early vegetative phenology in this coastal system. Temperature during May-July remained within the optimal range for rice vegetative growth (20-30Â°C), explaining its lack of predictive power for phenological variation. While temperature extremes can delay or accelerate crop development, the moderate maritime climate of the study region rarely imposes thermal constraints during the early season. Similarly, precipitation showed no significant relationship with U90, reflecting the irrigated nature of the production system, where water supply depends on managed reservoir releases rather than rainfall. The irrigation infrastructure effectively decouples crop water availability from precipitation variability, concentrating environmental risk onto water quality (salinity) rather than quantity. Vapor pressure deficit, an indicator of atmospheric water demand, likewise showed minimal influence, likely because flooded rice cultivation maintains high humidity in the crop microenvironment regardless of regional atmospheric conditions. This pattern of negative results for conventional environmental drivers strengthens the interpretation that salinity represents the dominant constraint in this system, identifying a clear target for diagnostic monitoring and potential intervention. The dominance of a single environmental factor during a specific growth window validates the diagnostic framework's core premise that ecological controls can be temporally and mechanistically isolated through phenology-based analysis.
