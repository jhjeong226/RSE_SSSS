## Manuscript

This study established a diagnostic framework linking satellite-based phenological metrics to environmental controls and spatial vulnerability patterns in a salinity-affected coastal rice system. Addressing our first research question, we identified the U90-D50 duration (90% green-up to 50% senescence) as the optimal yield indicator, explaining up to 76% of variance for spatially uniform cultivars compared to only 6% for traditional peak vegetation indices. This superior performance stems from quantifying reproductive stage duration rather than capturing instantaneous canopy state. However, the framework's effectiveness depends critically on spatial uniformity within cultivation areas. Cultivars exhibiting high phenological heterogeneity (standard deviation exceeding 6 days) showed weak duration-yield relationships, revealing that adequate spatial sampling represents a prerequisite for reliable satellite-based estimation. For the second research question on temporal environmental controls, systematic analysis of 20 monthly variables across 23 years revealed May-July salinity as the dominant driver of growth onset timing (U90), with correlations reaching r = 0.82 in recent years. This temporal specificity identifies actionable intervention windows. In contrast, senescence timing (D50) exhibited complex multi-factor controls, reflecting accumulated seasonal effects and management interactions rather than single environmental constraints. Addressing the third research question, spatial analysis successfully compartmentalized chronic vulnerability, with southern regions experiencing shortened durations in over 40% of years. Attribution analysis linking delayed-U90 zones with duration-vulnerable zones confirmed early-season salinity as the causal mechanism in these areas, mediated by infrastructure-dependent irrigation water quality that varies from 1738 ppm upstream to 2438 ppm downstream along the source reservoir.
