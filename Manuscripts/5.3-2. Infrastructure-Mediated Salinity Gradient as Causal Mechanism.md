## Manuscript

The infrastructure-mediated salinity distribution provides a mechanistic explanation for observed vulnerability patterns. Monitoring within the source reservoir revealed a substantial spatial salinity gradient exceeding 40%, with downstream locations exhibiting markedly higher salinity than upstream areas. This gradient arises from the reservoir's position in a coastal estuarine system, where saltwater intrusion affects southern portions closer to tidal influence more severely than northern reaches. The five major pumping stations drawing irrigation water from different locations along this gradient consequently deliver water of varying quality to spatially distinct field zones. Fields served by downstream stations receive chronically elevated salinity during the critical early-season growth window, systematically imposing stress that delays vegetative development. This infrastructure-environment coupling creates a fixed spatial template for vulnerability that operates independently of management decisions regarding cultivar selection or planting timing. The correspondence between pumping station coverage areas and identified vulnerability zones confirms that irrigation system configuration represents a primary structural determinant of field-level environmental stress exposure.
