## Manuscript

The remote sensing approach for phenology monitoring offers distinct advantages over traditional ground-based methods for operational agricultural applications. Satellite platforms provide systematic temporal coverage throughout the growing season, enabling detection of phenological transitions that field surveys might miss between discrete observation dates. The spatial continuity of satellite data further permits analysis across entire landscapes, revealing heterogeneity that point-based measurements cannot capture. For the 610-hectare study area, field surveys would require extensive labor to monitor phenological progression across hundreds of distinct field units, whereas satellite data provides comprehensive coverage at sub-field resolution every few days. This temporal frequency proves particularly valuable for capturing the onset and duration of specific phenological stages, which can shift substantially depending on environmental conditions and management timing. The multi-year consistency of satellite observations additionally enables retrospective analysis, allowing phenology-environment relationships to be established across diverse seasonal conditions that would be impractical to sample through intensive field campaigns. These operational advantages position satellite phenology as a scalable monitoring approach suitable for regional agricultural management systems where field-level information requirements exceed the capacity of conventional survey methods.
