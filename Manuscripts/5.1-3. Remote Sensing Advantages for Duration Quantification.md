## Manuscript

Satellite-based Land Surface Phenology provides distinct advantages for operationalizing duration-based yield estimation across heterogeneous landscapes. Traditional ground-based phenology monitoring, while accurate at point locations, cannot capture the spatial variability at the landscape scale required for regional crop management. Remote sensing overcomes this limitation through systematic temporal sampling and spatial coverage. The high temporal resolution of modern satellite systems (2-3 days for HLS, daily for MODIS) enables reliable detection of phenological transition timing through curve-fitting algorithms applied to vegetation index time series. This temporal density ensures that rapid phenological changes, particularly during critical stages like flowering and senescence onset, are adequately captured despite cloud contamination or atmospheric interference. Furthermore, the spatial consistency of satellite observations eliminates observer bias and methodological variations inherent in ground surveys. Our demonstration that U90-D50 duration maintains strong correlations across multiple years and cultivars (r > 0.85 for GQ and DJ) confirms the operational robustness of satellite-derived phenological metrics. This consistency enables transition from site-specific research applications to operational yield forecasting systems deployable across extensive agricultural regions.
