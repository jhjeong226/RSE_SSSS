## Manuscript

The identified vulnerability patterns inform both strategic infrastructure planning and tactical field-level management decisions. At the strategic level, the clear spatial signature of infrastructure-mediated vulnerability identifies high-return investment opportunities. Improving water quality delivered by downstream pumping stations would ameliorate chronic stress across extensive vulnerable zones, offering greater aggregate benefit than dispersed field-level interventions. This might involve developing alternative low-salinity water sources for specific stations, implementing blending systems that dilute high-salinity water with better-quality sources, or prioritizing desalinization infrastructure at critical supply points. At the tactical level, field operators can adapt management strategies to acknowledge fixed vulnerability patterns. Fields in chronically stressed zones might receive preferential allocation of salt-tolerant cultivars, earlier transplanting to allow longer vegetative growth periods, or enhanced nutrient management to compensate for salinity-impaired nutrient uptake. The phenology-based diagnostic framework thus enables both corrective infrastructure investments targeting root causes and adaptive field management accommodating persistent environmental constraints. This dual-level application potential distinguishes diagnostic approaches from purely predictive models that quantify outcomes without revealing actionable intervention opportunities.
