## Manuscript

The persistence of spatial vulnerability patterns across years of varying cultivar allocation demonstrates that environmental constraints dominate management flexibility in determining field-level productivity. While cultivar selection, planting timing, and agronomic practices influence outcomes within individual seasons, the spatial template of vulnerability remains fixed because it reflects immutable infrastructure configuration and environmental gradients. This environmental determinism indicates that ameliorating chronic vulnerability requires infrastructure-level interventions addressing water quality at source or delivery rather than field-level management refinements. The strong spatial correspondence between vulnerability zones and irrigation infrastructure coverage suggests specific intervention points. Downstream pumping stations delivering elevated-salinity water create vulnerability across all fields they serve, regardless of cultivar or management. Addressing salinity at these critical nodes through alternative water sources, blending strategies, or improved conveyance infrastructure could benefit entire zones simultaneously. This systems-level perspective contrasts with field-specific management approaches that might achieve incremental improvements but cannot overcome fundamental environmental constraints imposed by irrigation water quality.
