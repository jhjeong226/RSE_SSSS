## Manuscript

Senescence timing in field-grown rice emerges from the accumulated effects of environmental conditions, developmental programming, and resource status throughout the reproductive phase. Unlike early vegetative transitions that respond primarily to immediate environmental stimuli, late-season phenology integrates the plant's entire developmental history and current physiological state. Multiple environmental pathways can lead to similar senescence timing through equifinality, where different combinations of temperature, water stress, nutrient availability, and pest pressure produce equivalent outcomes. For instance, moderate salinity stress combined with optimal temperatures might induce similar senescence timing as low salinity with cooler temperatures, because both scenarios result in comparable rates of reproductive development and grain filling completion. Management interventions additionally interact with environmental drivers, as irrigation adjustments, nutrient applications, or pest control measures can partially compensate for environmental stresses and alter their phenological effects. This multivariate complexity explains why single-factor correlations remain weak and inconsistent across time periods despite clear mechanistic linkages between environment and senescence in controlled studies. The satellite phenology approach captures this complexity by quantifying the final integrated outcome across all contributing factors, providing a holistic measure of reproductive phase duration that proves more robust for yield prediction than attempting to model individual environmental pathways.
